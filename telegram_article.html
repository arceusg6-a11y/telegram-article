📋 The Crime Scene — Where Did This Money Come From?

A "Free App" Just Paid Off $2.35 Billion. How?

Let's rewind. In 2021, Telegram was essentially broke. The app was completely free, it had no real revenue model, and it was burning cash just to keep the servers running. So what did founder Pavel Durov do? He issued bonds — essentially borrowed $2.35 billion from investors at 7% annual interest, promising to pay it all back by March 2026.

Everyone thought it was a gamble. A free messaging app borrowing billions? With no clear path to profit? Many investors were skeptical. Bond yields spiked to 17% after Durov was arrested in Paris in 2024 — meaning the market thought there was a serious chance Telegram couldn't pay it back. But this week, Durov confirmed: every single dollar has been repaid. Full stop. So what changed? Everything.

🕵️ The 5 Moves That Made This Possible

How Telegram Went From Broke To $540M Profit

MOVE 01

⭐ Telegram Premium — The Game Changer

In 2022, Telegram launched Telegram Premium — a paid subscription giving users extra features. It sounds simple. But the execution was brilliant. By 2025, 13 million users were paying an average of $2.80 per month. That's over $436 million annually from subscriptions alone. And if they ever reach just 3% of their 1 billion users? That's $1 billion per year from subscriptions alone. The free app found its golden goose.

MOVE 02

📢 Advertising Revenue — From $0 to $245M

In 2023, Telegram's ad revenue was $100 million. By 2024, it had jumped to $245 million — and projections for 2025 were $350 million. How? Telegram introduced video ads in public channels, similar to YouTube's model. Viewers who don't want ads can pay for Premium. It's the same playbook YouTube used — and it's working at massive scale with 500 million daily active users.

MOVE 03

🪙 Toncoin & Crypto — The Hidden Revenue Stream

Telegram is deeply tied to TON blockchain. Premium subscriptions and in-app purchases run through TON. Telegram sold over $450 million worth of Toncoin. The Fragment marketplace — where you buy username handles on the blockchain — generated enormous fees. Most people think of Telegram as just a messaging app. In reality, it's quietly building a crypto-powered financial ecosystem inside your phone.

MOVE 04

🏦 BlackRock & Citadel Backed Them

When Telegram issued new bonds in 2025 worth $1.7 billion, the investors who showed up were BlackRock — the world's largest asset manager — Abu Dhabi's sovereign wealth fund Mubadala, and hedge fund giant Citadel. These are not people who invest in dying companies. When the smartest money in the world lines up to lend you $1.7 billion, it means they see something the public hasn't priced in yet. That something is Telegram's IPO.

MOVE 05

📈 From Loss to Profit in One Year

In 2023, Telegram lost $173 million. In 2024, it made $540 million in profit on $1.4 billion in revenue. That's a swing of over $700 million in a single year. For context — that's a bigger turnaround than most publicly listed companies manage in 5 years. And for 2025, projections are over $700 million in profit. The company that couldn't afford its own servers in 2020 is now one of the most profitable tech companies per user on the planet.

We built an innovative monetization strategy starting in 2021 and reached profitability in 2024. As a result, our new bond offering last year was oversubscribed.

— Pavel Durov, Telegram CEO & Founder

Telegram was valued at over $30 billion by potential investors before the IPO was delayed. With $1.4 billion in revenue and $540 million in profit — and growing — that valuation could be significantly higher when they finally go public. The investors in the new 2025 bonds can convert them to equity at 80% of the IPO price. Meaning BlackRock, Citadel, and Mubadala are already positioned to make enormous returns the moment Telegram lists.
